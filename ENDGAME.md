# Dead Mall Tycoon — Endgame Vision

The game is finished when a player, forty minutes into a run, can experience the following:

A massive mall — wings stretching in multiple directions, multiple anchors, dozens of storefronts. All but one closed. The Sears terminal is a dead end, boarded, signage removed. JCPenney is gone too — replaced by a vast quiet where an anchor used to be. The east wing is sealed entirely; the north wing is a curated graveyard of display spaces showing vintage mall photos, a historical plaque, a local artist's installation.

One tenant remains — a pretzel kiosk, or a nail salon, or a pay phone repair shop. It has been there for fourteen years. It is the only thing generating rent.

The lights are dim. Fluorescent fixtures flicker in slow, uneven rhythm. The corridor has a blue-green cast. Cracked tile and water-stained ceiling overlays stretch across the floor and above. Somewhere, a kugel ball sits motionless in dry water, pennies still visible at the bottom.

Vaporwave plays softly — chopped muzak from 1985, pitched down, heavy reverb. Underneath it, the fluorescent hum is louder than the music.

One visitor walks through the mall. Linda Hansen, 71, The Originals cohort. She stops at a display space showing photographs of the mall's 1982 grand opening. Her thought appears: "I brought my kids here the day it opened. The fountain worked then." The fountain is an artifact with memory weight 847.

The mall has not been bankrupt for seven in-game years. It somehow, through the player's careful curation of sealed spaces, display installations, and preserved artifacts, stays above the bottom line. Each month, the player places a new decoration, seals a storefront, converts another space to display. These actions are now the primary score events. The mall is scoring more per month than it did in its thriving years.

The ledger, scrolled from top to bottom, tells the story of this mall: every tenant that left, every artifact that decayed, every wing that went dark, every visitor that remembered something specific. The run is legible as a narrative, not a scoreboard.

When the player finally closes this mall — by choice, or by bankruptcy, or by the mall forgetting itself — the end screen shows the ledger in full. The score is a footnote. The story is the game.

This is what the game is for. Every design decision, every tuning pass, every prompt in the sequence is evaluated against this target: does this move us toward the mall described above, or away from it?

This document is not speculative. It is operational. Update it if the vision sharpens further, but do not dilute it. When in doubt during future development, re-read it. When in doubt during playtesting, evaluate against it. When in doubt during scoping decisions, ask: does this serve the endgame scene?
